Neocaridina-davidi

This genus of dwarf freshwater shrimp includes varieties such as Blue Jelly, Green Jade, Sunkist and Rili. But it is commonly known as the cherry shrimp.
this Species is popular amongst beginner shrimp keepers as they are both beautiful hardy and quite easy to breed and in this guide,
you will get needed knowledge to get started as a shrimp keeper.

Equipment

The equipment required to keep shrimp is pretty minimal, but it’s best to have everything on hand before you start to add the invertebrates. You should have the following:

  • An Aquarium 45+ liters
  • Shrimp safe Filter e.g. (Aquael PAT MINI & Eheim mini up)
  • Heater, not not strictly necessary if your room temperture is above 18° Celcius
  • Light. Read more on lights here
  • Substrate
  • Plants Suitable for Shrimp
  • Hardscape Pieces (Rocks and/or Driftwood)
  • Aquarium Stand or Suitable Place
  • Aquarium Test Kit

Get all of this together before you begin setting up.

Substrate

With Aquarium Substrate, we have to consider either going with an inert or active substrate. An inert substrate will not affect our water parameters but will require more supplementation to maintain plants. An active substrate buffers the waters PH/Kh. You will have more success with active substrate growing rooted plants as nutrients will be available through the substrate. If you are going with an active substrate, some good choices are Tropica Aquarium Soil, ADA Aquasoil or Fluval Stratum. Read more on substrate here

Plants

Featuring live plants in your aquarium not only makes it more attractive but is much healthier for your aquarium's inhabitants. The biggest benefit that live plants provide for your aquarium is that they produce oxygen (O2) and absorb ammonia (NH3) that your fish generate. Adding live plants in your aquarium helps recreate a natural ecosystem in miniature, and maybe one of the most beneficial ways to keep your fish healthy.
Plants provide shelter and security for the fish. Because they compete with algae for nutrients, they can help to reduce algae growth. Live plants enhance the appearance and provide a much more natural environment for the fish. By improving water quality and reducing stress, live plants are a great way to improve your fishes' health. When first adding live plants to your aquarium, it is wise to choose a large number of hardy plants such as Sagittaria, java fern , or Moneywort. As you gain more confidence and experience, you can start including the more sensitive plant species.

Water parameters

Recommended Tank Parameters

  • pH level range: 6.4 to 8.0, ideal range: 6.8 to 7.5
  • Temperature range: 18° to 25° Celcius
  • Water type: kH 0-10; gH 4-14; TDS 80-200

A small note:
All dwarf shrimp prefer to live in tanks with live aquatic plants there are a few reasons for this:

  • 1. Dwarf shrimp love the cover that plants provide them
  • 2. They love to graze on the plants for algae
  • 3. Plants help keep the water clean for the shrimp

Red Cherry Shrimp, though they are extremely adaptable and will thrive in a pH of 6.4 to 8.0, I have found that the optimum pH is between 6.8 and 7.5. Water temperature can be anywhere from 18° to 27° C, but the fastest breeding occurs at about 24° C.

AquaScaping

Simplicity
Aquascaping is all about taste and usually, less elements is more. Very often people are tempted to incorporate as many types of plants as possible, thinking that this would ensure a great visual variety, but most of the times the result is the opposite. Variety
Keeping it simple does not mean using one type of plant only. Even if your intention is to create a theme, you don’t want your aquascape to look boring. Remember, imagination plays a key role in aquascaping! Proportion
It’s very important to give a sense of harmony to your tank, so try to have as much open space as filled space. Avoid using only large leaf plants because they take from the proportion and depth of your aquascape. Persistence
Aquascaping can become frustrating, be sure of that! So be ready to deconstruct and reconstruct if there’s something you don’t like about your aquascape. The more you experiment, the better you will get at it. Read more about aquascaping here
